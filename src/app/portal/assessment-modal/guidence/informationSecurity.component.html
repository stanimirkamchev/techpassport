Background:

All suppliers are expected to have a mature and active Information Security postition and as such this is section is relevant to all. If the supplier is likely to handle bank data such as credit cards and/or Personally Identifiable Information (PII) then addtional addtional controls and accurances will be required.
  • Classification, Retention and handling of your data - This shows that you gave identified the information you hold and how you handle it to maintain confidentiality.
  • Change Management – To show you have a robust change management system to avoid issues with availability.
  • Incident Management – A fully tested incident management process shows banks that if an issue was to arise it would be dealt with promptly, in an organised way and integrity would be restored to systems in a timely manner.
  • Anti-Virus measures – This displays the supplier protects its systems from potential loss of confidentiality, integrity and availability.
  • Patch Management – This is critical to stopping attacks against systems and could be linked to the failure of confidentiality, integrity and availability.
  • Risk Management – The suppliers strong security posture relies on an active risk management system in place.
  • Security Awareness & Training – This is essential part of the security toolkit. A high level of attacks are now levelled at staff and effective training can reduce the chance of loss of confidentially.
  • Cryptography Control – Encryption is important to ensure the integrity of data
  • Firewalls – Firewalls are a crucial component to stop hackers from getting in and protecting different parts of your network.
  • Access Control & Password management – Only give the rights necessary and no more. Access control is very important to stop loss of confidentiality through users having more access than they need.
  • 3rd Parties – It’s essential 3rd parties understand the your risks and requirements are also theirs.
  • Secure Software Development – During software development security must be an integral element. Failure to do this may open vulnerabilities in your code.
  • Backups – Absolutely essential as a last port of call in a business continuity situation.
  • Data Loss Prevention – DLP is a function available in some software suites that identifies sensitive information as it moves around your systems and goes out. Appropriate use of DLP will uphold confidentially.
  • Monitoring – Without monitoring you may not be aware of an issue until it becomes critical and you won’t be able to track down the source of the issue. Monitoring upholds Confidentiality, integrity and availability.
  • Intrusion Detection Systems – IDS look for unusual behaviour and can provide early warning of a security breach.
  • Distributed Denial of Service - DDoS affects availability and therefore it is highly recommended to have preventative systems in place to avoid this scenario.
  • Vulnerability and Penetration testing – It is fundamental to have regular vulnerability and penetration testing to ensure issues are identified and resolved.
  • Data Privacy – compliance with GDPR is a legal requirement and is key to maintain confidentially of data
  • Screening – Screening of staff is important to avoid employing staff that could do you damage.

Title:
Information Security Piolicy
Description:
Your Information Security Policy (ISP) is your main document that sets out your security rules for your staff and the business as a whole. It must cover or make reference to sub-policies that explain all your rules in connection with Information Technology systems and digitally stored data. This document is your public security position and should viewed in this way. You may state all your security requirements within it however this can also be broken out into sub-policies which you reference within the ISP.
Guidance:
Information security is deemed to safeguard three main objectives:
  • Confidentiality – data and information assets must be confined to people authorized to access and not be disclosed to others;
  • Integrity – keeping the data intact, complete and accurate, and IT systems operational;
  • Availability – an objective indicating that information or system is at disposal of authorized users when needed.
When thinking about your ISP you should think about the following topics:
  • Classification, Retention and data handling
  • Change Management Systems
  • Incident Management Systems
  • Anti-Virus measures
  • Patch Management precautions
  • Risk Management framework
  • Security Awareness & Training
  • Cryptography Controls enforced
  • Firewalls installed
  • Access Control & Password enforcement
  • Management of 3rd Parties
  • Secure Software Development
  • Backup strategy
  • Data Loss Prevention
  • Monitoring
  • Intrusion Detection Systems
  • Distributed Denial of Service prevention
  • Vulnerability and Penetration testing schedule
  • Data Privacy controls
  • Staff Screening

Title:
Security Incident Management
Details:
The Supplier should provide their "Security Incident Management Procedure". This reports and manages all security incidents.
Guidance:
The Security Incident Management Procedure should detail the following:
  • Raising of Incidents
  • Triage of the Incident to identify its severity
  • Running of a scenario playbook to Prepare, Detect, Analyse, Contain, Eradicate and Recover
  • Lessons learnt
Title:
Change Management
Details:
The Supplier should provide details of their "Change Management Process".
Guidance:
The Change Management Process should detail the following:
  • Roles and responsibilities (the initiator should not be the approver)
  • The process itself
  • A sample change request form including:
    • Times and dates of change
    • Priority
    • Business reason for change
    • Detailed implementation plan
    • Testing plan
    • Backout plan
    • Asset Owner
    • Likelihood of services being impacted
    • Communications Plan
Title:
Vulnerability Management
Details:
The Supplier should provide their "Vulnerability Management Procedure". This provides details of how vulnerabilities are identified and resolved.
Guidance:
The Vulnerability Management Process should detail the following:
  • Discovery – vulnerabilities are discovered
  • Prioritisation – vulnerabilities are assessed and prioritised for remedial action
  • Planning – a plan is devised to mitigate the vulnerabilities
  • Remediation – the action of resolving the issues
  • Validation – checking the remedial actions have had the desired effect.
Title:
Data Security, Logical And Physical Access Control
Guidance:
The Supplier should not access Bank Infrastructure and/or Data on the Bank Infrastructure and/or access Bank owned assets without the prior written consent of the Bank. Where consent is given the following minimum security measures should be applied to ensure access to Bank Information is restricted to the minimum level necessary for individual role responsibilities to be discharged. The Supplier should ensure:
  • Supplier employees or sub-contractors use the Bank supplied Users IDs and that secret authentication information (e.g. passwords, PINs) and security tokens are protected and not shared with any other individuals;
  • Supplier employees or sub-contractors do not transmit Bank information from the Bank’s infrastructure to the Supplier’s infrastructure prior without written consent from the Bank;
  • Supplier employees or sub-contractors comply with Bank policies where supplied with Bank owned equipment; Processes (i.e. leavers process, recertfication) are in place to identify Supplier employees or sub-contractors who no longer require access (i.e. job role changes, leavers) to Bank systems;
  • Timely notification to the Bank of Supplier employees or sub-contractors who no longer need access to Bank systems to perform their role including the return of any Bank property, e.g. ID cards, laptop, secure token;
  • Supplier employees or sub-contractors are made aware that the Bank may monitor use Bank systems.
Title:
Storage And Destruction Of Bank Information
Guidance:
The Supplier should protect all Bank Information (held by Supplier Employees or Approved Subcontractors in any form) by adopting a ‘clear desk’ policy and disposing of Bank Information securely by treating it as confidential waste. The Supplier should ensure that any Bank Information held by the Supplier or an Approved Subcontractor is disposed of in a manner which protects the confidential nature of Bank Information.
Title:
Payment Card Industry (PCI) Terms And Conditions
Guidance:
Where the Services concern or relate to payment cards (including without limitation debit cards, credit cards or prepaid cards) the Supplier should comply with the Payment Card Industry Security Standards Council’s standards in respect of Cardholder Data (including without limitation PCI DSS (Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard), Payment Application, PA DSS (Payment Application Data Security Standard), PCI PIN Security Requirements, PCI Card Security Requirements etc (collectively, the ""PCI Standards"")). The Supplier should comply with the PCI Security Standards for the duration of the contract with the Bank and until such point as all relevant data (including Cardholder Data) has been returned, transferred or securely deleted (in accordance with PCI Standards) as directed by the Bank. The Supplier should hold PCI DSS Attestation of Compliance accreditation and advise the Bank immediately if this is lost. Where the Service includes the provision of any product supporting the processing of Cardholder Data then in addition to the above requirements the Payment Application should have been evaluated as PA DSS compliant and the specific version documented on the PCI Council list of validated Payment Applications.
Title:
Termination And Decommissioning
Guidance:
At the end of the Term (for whatever reason) the Supplier should ensure that Bank Information assets are returned or destroyed appropriately as agreed with the Bank. Any continued use of the information assets should be formally agreed with the Bank and covered by an appropriate contract.
